PTC University

EdTech · Global Accessibility & Localization

Learning platforms overwhelm with choice: 47 courses, 12 filter facets, zero guidance. Most learners abandon before they find anything. Discovery at global scale required a different approach.

Act I — A Scene

Amara, a vocational student in Lagos, opened the learning platform on her phone to find the next course in her engineering track. The homepage showed 47 recommended courses. She scrolled. And scrolled. None of them seemed right. She looked for a "filter" option. Found one. It had 12 different facets (product category, skill level, duration, learning format, instructor, language, certification, prerequisites, reviews, difficulty, format, and more). Overwhelmed, she closed the app. By the time she returned to find it, she'd forgotten what course she was looking for.

Act II — The World

PTC University serves 550,000+ learners across 80+ countries. From Fortune 500 engineers in Silicon Valley to vocational students in emerging markets. The platform contains thousands of courses—a beautiful knowledge base that also happens to be a labyrinth. The interface was built for the power user (experienced engineer, knows exactly what they want to learn, comfortable with advanced filters). It was hostile to the novice (new learner, exploring, vulnerable to decision paralysis, often on slow 3G connections).

Beyond discovery, the platform had a deeper problem. It treated accessibility as a box to check, not a foundation to build on. Keyboard navigation was afterthought. Localization for 11 languages was a translation-at-the-end problem. Mobile experience was a desktop-first design squeezed onto small screens. And none of this was working together—the cumulative friction meant learners like Amara were dropping off before they ever found what they needed.

Role: Lead product designer for platform redesign · Owned information architecture, learning paths, discovery, and accessibility · 2017–2019 · Served 550K+ users · 11 languages, 80+ countries

Act III — The Encounter

I interviewed 40 learners across 6 countries. The pattern was clear: experienced learners wanted power and filters. Novices wanted guidance and clarity. The old platform tried to give everyone everything, and ended up confusing both groups.

The insight came from a conversation with a translator on our team. She said: "When I localize the interface, I add 30% more characters in some languages. The design breaks. And I can't fix it—I can only translate." That's when I realized: accessibility and localization aren't separate problems. They're the same problem. Design for global accessibility first, and localization becomes easy. Ignore it, and you're fighting physics.

When you design for accessibility, you don't make the interface accessible. You make it simpler for everyone. Keyboard navigation is faster for power users. Clear hierarchy helps non-native speakers. Progressive enhancement works better on slow connections. You're not adding accessibility. You're removing friction.

Act IV — The Work

Progressive Disclosure for Discovery: Instead of showing 12 filters, I built a two-tier system. New learners start with simple options ("Skill level" and "Recommended for you"). As they gain confidence, they can expand to more specific filters. The interface grows with the learner, not against them. This is the same reason good books have tables of contents *and* chapters—different users have different entry points.

Localization-First Architecture: I structured the information architecture knowing it would be translated. Short labels. Clear hierarchy. Minimal text in images. Open space for text expansion (some languages need 30% more width than English). We tested each of the 11 languages early—week 3, not week 20. The translators' feedback informed the design, not the other way around.

Keyboard-First Navigation: Every interactive element was keyboard-navigable from day one. Skip links let keyboard users jump directly to content. Screen reader users got semantic headings, proper landmarks, meaningful form labels. This wasn't bolted on in QA. It was part of the component system.

Mobile-first performance. We optimized for the 3G phones that most of our global learners used. Lazy-loaded images with low-quality placeholders. Progressive enhancement (works without JavaScript, then JavaScript enhances). Cached content. The core learning experience loaded in under 2 seconds on a 3G connection instead of 10.

Act V — The Turn

We launched the redesigned platform to 550k+ users across 11 languages. Course enrollment jumped 28%. Completion rates improved 15%. Mobile usage (especially in emerging markets) jumped 40%. Support tickets for "how do I find a course?" dropped 60%.

But the most important metric: accessibility compliance. We exceeded WCAG AA standards. 99% of automated accessibility tests passed. Manual testing confirmed keyboard and screen reader usability. This wasn't because we were "doing good." It was because building accessibility-first meant we'd eliminated friction for everyone—including people who didn't have an accessibility need but still benefited from simpler, faster interactions. (550K+ users · 28% enrollment lift · 15% completion improvement · 40% mobile growth · WCAG AA+ compliance · 11 languages, 80+ countries)

Act VI — The Implication

This project proved something that changed how I think about design: accessibility isn't a separate track. It's the right design. A platform optimized for screen readers is also optimized for clarity. A platform designed for keyboard navigation is also faster for power users. A platform built for localization is more modular, more maintainable, more resilient.

Design Patterns Demonstrated

This learning platform became a study in two core patterns for designing at global scale:

  • AI Failure States: When the adaptive recommendation system couldn't determine a clear path (low confidence, new learner profile, conflicting signals), we designed graceful degradation: show the most popular course instead, with clear reasoning about why we fell back to this pattern.
  • Human-in-Loop Patterns: Learners could override the system's recommendations. A learner could skip courses, take them in a different order, or request a manual counselor review. These override mechanisms were essential for building trust in the adaptive path.

I've applied this principle to every intelligent system I've designed since: make it simple, make it work globally, make it accessible. Not as three separate projects. As one. The result is systems that serve not just the majority, but the margins. And paradoxically, systems that are better for everyone.

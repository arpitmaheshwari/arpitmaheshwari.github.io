O2 Priority

Telecom · Billing Transparency

Act I — A Scene

Rebecca opened her phone bill and saw a charge of £47 for roaming. She'd spent three days in Spain. Immediately, she thought: was she overcharged? Did they count that airport layover wrongly? She called customer support. After 15 minutes on hold, the agent explained: "You used 2.1GB of data at £0.50 per megabyte. That's the roaming rate for Spain." Rebecca did the math mentally. Actually... that was right. She wasn't overcharged. But she had to call support to know that. The number alone told her nothing. It wasn't that the bill was opaque. It was that it was untranslatable.

Act II — The World

O2's Priority tier customers (premium subscribers) were calling support constantly with the same question: "Why is my bill so high?" The previous billing portal showed everything—every call, every text, every megabyte of data. It was completely transparent. And completely useless because customers couldn't relate the numbers to their actual behavior.

Support tickets for "bill explanation" cost £8 per call. With thousands of calls monthly, and the actual problem being interface design rather than billing error, O2 knew something had to change. But how do you make a complex billing system feel trustworthy when even showing all the information wasn't working?

Role: Lead product designer for Priority tier customer portal · Redesigned billing and usage interface · 2014–2016 · Served millions of UK subscribers

Act III — The Encounter

I conducted research with 50 customers. The pattern was clear: they didn't distrust the bill because information was hidden. They distrusted it because they couldn't *verify it.* When you see a charge without context—without knowing what behavior triggered it, what rate was applied, when the event occurred—you have no way to evaluate whether it's correct.

But when I showed a customer a roaming charge contextualized as "Spain: 2.1GB at £0.50/MB = £42 + £5 roaming fee = £47," something shifted. They might still think it's expensive. But they trust it. They can do the math. They can relate it back to their trip.

Transparency isn't about showing every number. It's about helping people understand which numbers matter and why they matter. Context is the bridge between data and trust.

Act IV — The Work

Bill Summary with Category Breakdown: The bill no longer opened with a dense table. It started with a clear summary: "Your £67 bill includes: Subscription (£35), Data overages (£18), International calls (£8), Roaming (£6)." One glance told the story. Each category was expandable to drill into detail.

Usage-to-Cost Visualization: For data charges, we created a chart showing the customer's daily data usage throughout the month. A highlighted line showed their plan limit. The visualization answered the question immediately: "When did I exceed my limit? How much overage am I paying for?" Behavior became visible, and cost became a logical consequence of that behavior.

Per-Item Context and Math: Every roaming charge, every international call, every service fee came with its calculation. "Call to US: 14 minutes at £0.35/minute = £4.90." Customers could verify the math. They could check the rate. Charges became auditable.

Predictive alerts and dispute mechanisms. We added intelligent warnings: "You're on track for £35 in data overage charges. [Upgrade plan] [Reduce usage]." And if a customer questioned a charge, one click opened a dispute form where they could explain. We tracked these disputes—not as customer complaints, but as signals about clarity gaps in the interface.

Act V — The Turn

Within six months of launch, billing support tickets dropped 40%. Not because we hid information, but because we made it transparent enough to understand. Customers used the billing portal 3.5× more often—not complaining, but checking their usage proactively using the predictive alerts. Disputes and chargebacks dropped 60% because customers could now verify for themselves that their bill was correct.

Most tellingly, Priority tier NPS (Net Promoter Score) improved 12 points. In telecom, that's massive. Customers still paid the same amount. The network wasn't faster. But they trusted their bills because they understood them. Rebecca never had to call support again because the bill itself told her story: when she roamed, how much data she used, and why the charge was what it was. (Global customers across UK · 40% support reduction · 3.5× portal engagement · 60% fewer disputes · +12 NPS improvement)

Act VI — The Implication

This project showed me that transparency in complex systems isn't about *showing more*. It's about *helping people understand what they're seeing*. Connect charges to behavior. Show the math. Provide context. That's when numbers become trustworthy.

I've carried this forward to every system where non-technical people need to understand complex decisions: AI recommendations, financial risk assessments, network performance, algorithmic pricing. In each case, the pattern holds. Show the logic. Make it verifiable. Give people the context to understand why something costs what it costs, why an algorithm recommended something, why a metric matters. That's when trust emerges from transparency.

How I Design Confidence Scores for AI Products

The bottleneck in AI adoption isn't the model—it's user confidence. When non-technical stakeholders see an AI recommendation, they ask: should I trust this? I design confidence scores as the primary interaction, not the secondary footnote.

Design the Failure State First

AI is wrong 30–70% of the time in real usage. The happy path demo is 30% of the design work. The failure state—when confidence is low, data is missing, or the model is uncertain—is the other 70%. This is what I design first.

Framework · AI Design

The Explainability Layer: Making AI Legible

Users don't trust black boxes. When an AI recommends something, I design explainability into the interface—showing confidence, data provenance, what the model weighted, why it could be wrong. Transparency is the product.

Pattern · Interaction Design

Human-in-the-Loop: When to Ask, When to Override

If an AI is right 99% of the time, making users click "confirm" 99% of the time is broken design. I design systems that only ask for confirmation when the AI is uncertain—respecting user time and building trust through earned authority.
